SECTION 01

HISTORY OF COMPUTING

From room-sized machines to quantum processors — the incredible evolution of computers.

1940s — The Dawn

ENIAC, the first general-purpose electronic computer, filled an entire room with 18,000 vacuum tubes and weighed 30 tons. It could perform 5,000 additions per second.

1970s — Microprocessor Revolution

Intel 4004 became the first commercially available microprocessor, cramming 2,300 transistors into a single chip. Personal computing was born.

1990s — The Internet Age

Tim Berners-Lee invented the World Wide Web, connecting billions of people. The dot-com era transformed computing from a tool into a global platform.

The physical components that power every digital experience.

Central Processing Unit

The brain of the computer. Modern CPUs contain billions of transistors and execute billions of instructions per second at speeds up to 6GHz.

Intel AMD ARM

Graphics Processing Unit

Specialized for parallel processing. GPUs render stunning graphics and now power AI training with thousands of cores working simultaneously.

NVIDIA AMD

Memory (RAM)

Volatile, high-speed memory that stores actively used data. DDR5 RAM can transfer data at speeds up to 6,400 MT/s with ultra-low latency.

DDR5 ECC

Storage (SSD/HDD)

NVMe SSDs read at 7,000 MB/s — 70x faster than HDDs. PCIe Gen5 drives push the boundaries of data transfer speed for blazing-fast boot times.

NVMe Gen5 7200 RPM

Motherboard

The central hub connecting all components. Modern boards feature PCIe 5.0, Wi-Fi 7, and sophisticated power delivery systems.

Power Supply

Converts AC to DC power. 80+ Titanium-rated PSUs achieve 96% efficiency, delivering stable power to hungry components.
